About Short Tons

Typical Use Cases

The short ton (also called US ton or net ton) is the standard ton measurement in the United States for commercial freight, construction materials, and industrial production. Trucking companies rate vehicle capacity in short tons, steel mills measure output in tons, and construction sites order concrete and aggregates by the ton. Coal, aggregates, scrap metal, and bulk commodities are almost universally traded in short tons within the US domestic market.

Historical Background

The short ton was standardized in the United States as exactly 2,000 pounds (907.18 kg) to simplify calculations in the decimal system. It's "short" compared to the British long ton (2,240 lbs) but remains the default "ton" in American commerce. The short ton became the US standard through the Weights and Measures Act, aligning with the American preference for base-10 calculations. Despite global metrication, the short ton remains deeply embedded in US industry and infrastructure.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many grams are in a short ton?

One short ton (US ton) equals 907,184.7 grams or 907.18 kilograms. This is exactly 2,000 pounds.

What's the difference between short tons, long tons, and metric tons?

A short ton (US) = 907.18kg, a long ton (UK) = 1016.05kg, and a metric ton = 1000kg. Short tons are about 10% lighter than metric tons and about 12% lighter than long tons.

Why does the US use short tons instead of metric tons?

Historical reasons and infrastructure costs. The entire US freight, construction, and manufacturing industry is built around short tons. Converting would require massive systemic changes to scales, trucks, pricing systems, and regulations.

Are shipping containers measured in short tons?

International shipping primarily uses metric tons, but US domestic trucking uses short tons. Container weight limits are typically specified in both units to accommodate different markets.

How does the short ton affect waste management and recycling?

US waste management and recycling industries operate entirely in short tons for landfill capacity, truck weight limits, and material pricing. Municipal solid waste facilities report annual tonnage to the EPA in short tons, while recycling centers pay for scrap metal and paper by the ton. A typical garbage truck can legally carry about 10 short tons before hitting weight restrictions. When calculating carbon footprint or diversion rates, using metric tons instead of short tons introduces a 10% error. Environmental reports must specify which ton standard they use, as international climate frameworks use metric tons while US regulations cite short tons, complicating cross-border compliance comparisons.
