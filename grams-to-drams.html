About Drams

Typical Use Cases

The dram is historically significant in pharmacy and apothecary measurements, where medications were traditionally measured in drams and grains. Today, it's occasionally used in mixology for precise cocktail ingredient measurements, particularly in craft distilleries and historical recipe recreation. The dram is also encountered in vintage cookbooks and historical documents, making this conversion useful for culinary historians and enthusiasts of traditional preparation methods.

Historical Background

The dram derives from the ancient Greek "drachma," a silver coin and unit of weight. In the apothecaries' system, one dram equaled 1/8 of an apothecary ounce, while in the avoirdupois system (used here), it equals 1/16 of a standard ounce (1.7718 grams). Though largely replaced by metric measurements in modern pharmacy, the term "dram" persists in colloquial use—particularly in Scotland, where a "wee dram" refers to a small pour of whisky, and in historical pharmaceutical references.

Conversion Tips

When converting grams to drams for historical recipes, be aware that measurements may refer to either apothecary drams or avoirdupois drams, which differ slightly. This converter uses the avoirdupois dram (1.7718g), the more common modern standard. For pharmaceutical conversions, always verify whether historical references use apothecary or avoirdupois measures. In mixology, precision matters less than in medicine, but a general rule: 5 grams ≈ 3 drams, useful for scaling classic cocktail recipes.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many grams are in a dram?

One dram (avoirdupois) equals 1.7718452 grams. This is the standard dram used in most modern contexts, equal to 1/16 of an ounce.

What's the difference between apothecary and avoirdupois drams?

Apothecary drams (used historically in pharmacy) equal 3.8879 grams, while avoirdupois drams (used in general commerce) equal 1.7718 grams. This converter uses avoirdupois drams, the more common modern standard.

Is the dram still used in medicine?

No, modern pharmacy has almost entirely switched to the metric system (milligrams, grams). Drams appear only in historical medical texts or when studying traditional pharmaceutical formulations.

Why do bartenders mention drams for whisky?

In Scotland and Ireland, a "dram" colloquially refers to a pour of whisky, typically 25-35ml. This usage is traditional and doesn't correspond to the precise weight measurement of 1.7718 grams.
